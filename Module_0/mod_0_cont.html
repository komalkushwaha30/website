Module 0 :- Basic Math

Topics :-

1. Long Division (Decimal division and decimal multiplication, division when divisor is greater than dividend) :-

Long division is a method used to divide numbers, including decimals. When dividing by a decimal, move the decimal point in both the divisor and dividend to align them. Then proceed with the division as usual, placing the decimal point in the quotient above the original decimal position.

2. BODMAS :-

BODMAS is an acronym that helps us remember the correct sequence for solving mathematical expressions involving multiple operators. Each letter represents a specific operation: (i) B: Brackets (Numbers inside brackets are evaluated first).
(ii) O: Orders (Refers to powers or roots, such as exponents or indices).
(iii) D: Division (Perform division operations).
(iv) M: Multiplication (Carry out multiplication operations).
(v) A: Addition (Compute addition operations).
(vi) S: Subtraction (Calculate subtraction operations).

3. Algebra :-

Algebra is a fundamental branch of mathematics that deals with symbolic representation and the manipulation of mathematical expressions. It introduces variables (like x, y, and z) and extends arithmetic beyond basic addition and multiplication. In algebra, we use equations to express relationships between quantities, solve unknowns, and analyze patterns. From solving elementary equations to exploring abstract concepts, algebra plays a crucial role in various fields. Whether you’re finding the volume of a container or calculating bank interest, algebra provides the tools to express and solve mathematical problems.

4. Numbers (Even, Odd, Prime, Composite, Natural, etc.) :-

(i) Natural Numbers: Natural numbers are the positive integers we encounter while counting. They start from 1 and continue indefinitely: 1, 2, 3, 4, and so on.
(ii) Even Numbers: Even numbers are integers that are divisible by 2. Their unit place digits can be 0, 2, 4, 6, or 8. Examples include 8, 14, and 50.
(iii) Odd Numbers: Unlike even numbers, odd numbers are not divisible by 2. They have 1, 3, 5, 7, or 9 at their unit place. Examples include 13, 21, and 65.
(iv) Prime Numbers: Prime numbers have exactly two factors: 1 and the number itself. Examples include 2, 3, 5, and 7. Every prime number is odd except for the number 2.
(v) Composite Numbers: Composite numbers are non-prime natural numbers. They have more than two factors. For instance, 4, 6, and 9 are composite numbers.

5. HCF & LCM :-

(i) HCF (Highest Common Factor): The HCF, also known as the greatest common factor (GCF), represents the largest positive integer that divides two or more numbers without leaving a remainder. For example, consider the numbers 8 and 12. The H.C.F. of 8 and 12 is 4 because it’s the highest number that can divide both 8 and 12 evenly.
(ii)LCM (Least Common Multiple): The LCM is the smallest positive integer that is divisible by two or more given numbers. For instance, let’s take the positive integers 4 and 6. The common multiples for 4 and 6 are 12, 24, 36, and so on. The least common multiple among these is 12. To find the LCM of any two numbers, we can use methods like prime factorization or division.

6. Factorization in algebra :-

Factorization involves expressing a given algebraic expression as a product of its factors. These factors can be numbers, variables, or other algebraic expressions. The process of factorization simplifies expressions, helps solve equations, and aids in understanding mathematical relationships. For instance, consider the quadratic expression (x^2 - 4). It can be factorized as ((x - 2)(x + 2)), where both ((x - 2)) and ((x + 2)) are factors.

7. Quadratic Equation (Finding roots) :-

(i) Quadratic Equation: A quadratic equation is a polynomial equation of degree two. It takes the form: (ax^2 + bx + c = 0), where (a), (b), and (c) are constants, and (x) represents the variable. Quadratic equations often arise in problems related to motion, geometry, and science.
(ii) Roots of a Quadratic Equation: The roots of a quadratic equation are the values of (x) that satisfy the equation, making it equal to zero. These roots can be found using various methods: Factoring: Factor the quadratic expression and set each factor equal to zero. Quadratic Formula: Use the formula: (x = \frac{{-b \pm \sqrt{{b^2 - 4ac}}}}{{2a}}). Completing the Square: Transform the equation into a perfect square trinomial. Graphing: Locate the x-intercepts on a graph. Example: Consider the quadratic equation: (x^2 - 7x + 10 = 0). By factoring, we find the roots: (x = 2) and (x = 5), as they satisfy the equation.

8. Logarithm & exponentiation :-

(i)Logarithms: Logarithms are the inverse of exponentiation. They provide a different way to express exponents. Given a base (b) and a number (x), the logarithm of (x) to the base (b) is the exponent to which (b) must be raised to yield (x).
For example: If (10^2 = 100), then (\log_{10} 100 = 2).
In general, (\log_b x = n) means (b^n = x).
Logarithms help simplify large numbers and have applications in various fields.
(ii)Exponentiation: Exponentiation involves raising a base number to a certain power (exponent).
For instance: (2^3 = 8) (2 raised to the power of 3 equals 8). Exponents allow us to express repeated multiplication efficiently. Logarithms and exponentiation are closely related: logarithms tell us how many times a base must be multiplied to get a specific number, while exponentiation represents the process of repeated multiplication.
